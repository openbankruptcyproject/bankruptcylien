Liens in Bankruptcy

A plain-language guide to lien stripping, cramdown, and lien avoidance - the tools bankruptcy law provides to deal with secured claims on your property.

What Is a Lien?

A lien is a creditor's legal claim against your property. It gives the creditor the right to take or sell that property if you do not pay the underlying debt. Liens come in many forms: mortgage liens on your home, security interests on your car, tax liens from the IRS or state government, judgment liens from lawsuits, and mechanic's liens from contractors.

Liens are created in different ways. Consensual liens arise from agreements you sign, like a mortgage or car loan. Statutory liens are imposed by law, such as tax liens or mechanic's liens. Judicial liens result from court judgments - when a creditor sues you, wins, and records the judgment against your property.

Understanding the type of lien matters because bankruptcy treats each type differently. Consensual liens on your primary residence receive strong protection under the Bankruptcy Code. Judicial liens that impair your exemptions can be removed entirely. And liens on personal property like cars can sometimes be reduced to the property's current market value.

In Rem vs. In Personam: Why This Distinction Matters

The most important concept for understanding liens in bankruptcy is the difference between in rem and in personam liability. In personam liability is your personal obligation to pay a debt - the promise you made when you signed the loan agreement. In rem liability is the creditor's right to look to specific property for payment - the lien itself.

A bankruptcy discharge eliminates in personam liability. After discharge, you are no longer personally obligated to pay the debt. The creditor cannot sue you, garnish your wages, or call you demanding payment. But the discharge does not, by itself, eliminate in rem rights. The lien remains attached to the property.

This means that after a Chapter 7 discharge, a mortgage lender cannot sue you for a deficiency if you walk away from the house - but the lien remains on the house. If you want to keep the property, you generally need to keep paying. If you stop paying, the lender can foreclose on the lien, even though your personal liability was discharged.

How Bankruptcy Can Modify Liens

While discharge alone does not eliminate liens, the Bankruptcy Code provides several specific mechanisms to modify, reduce, or remove liens entirely. These tools are among the most powerful benefits of filing bankruptcy, and they vary by chapter and by the type of property and lien involved:

  • Lien stripping - Removing junior mortgages on underwater homes in Chapter 13
  • Cramdown - Reducing a secured claim to the current value of the collateral under Section 506(a)
  • Lien avoidance - Removing judicial liens that impair exemptions under Section 522(f)
  • Redemption - Paying the current value of personal property in a lump sum to eliminate the lien under Section 722 (Chapter 7 only)
  • Trustee avoidance powers - The bankruptcy trustee can avoid certain liens as preferences (Section 547) or fraudulent transfers (Section 548)

Each of these tools has specific requirements, limitations, and procedures. The pages linked below explain each one in detail.

Types of Liens in Bankruptcy

Consensual Liens

Consensual liens are created voluntarily when you pledge property as collateral for a loan. The two most common examples are mortgage liens (where the lien is on your home) and purchase money security interests (where the lien is on a car, furniture, or other property you bought with the loan proceeds).

Consensual liens receive significant protection under the Bankruptcy Code. Section 1322(b)(2) - the anti-modification clause - prohibits Chapter 13 plans from modifying the rights of holders of claims secured only by the debtor's principal residence. This means you generally cannot cram down your first mortgage in Chapter 13. However, junior mortgages that are wholly unsecured (because the home's value does not reach the balance on the first mortgage) can be stripped.

For personal property like cars, consensual liens can be crammed down to the property's value - subject to the 910-day hanging paragraph for recently purchased vehicles. See Cramdown for details.

Judicial Liens

Judicial liens arise when a creditor obtains a court judgment against you and records it against your property. These liens are the most vulnerable in bankruptcy because Section 522(f) allows debtors to avoid judicial liens that impair exemptions. If a judgment lien on your home prevents you from claiming your full homestead exemption, you can ask the bankruptcy court to remove it entirely.

The key distinction is that judicial liens are involuntary - you did not agree to pledge your property as collateral. Congress determined that these involuntary liens should not defeat the fresh start that bankruptcy exemptions are designed to provide. See Lien Avoidance Under Section 522(f) for the formula and procedure.

Statutory Liens

Statutory liens arise automatically by operation of law, without any court action or voluntary agreement. The most common examples include tax liens (federal and state), mechanic's liens (from contractors who performed work on your property), and landlord's liens (in some states). Statutory liens generally cannot be avoided under Section 522(f), which is limited to judicial liens and certain nonpossessory, nonpurchase money security interests.

Tax liens, in particular, are difficult to deal with in bankruptcy. The IRS can have both a secured claim (to the extent of the lien) and a priority unsecured claim (for recent taxes). A Chapter 13 plan must pay priority tax claims in full, and tax liens pass through bankruptcy unless the underlying tax debt is both dischargeable and paid through the plan.

How Liens Interact with Exemptions

Exemptions are the assets that bankruptcy law allows you to keep - your home equity (up to a limit), your car, household goods, retirement accounts, and other necessities. When a lien exists on exempt property, it can prevent you from receiving the full benefit of the exemption. This is why exemption law and lien law are deeply interconnected.

If you own a home worth $200,000 with a $150,000 mortgage and a $30,000 judgment lien, your equity is $20,000. If your state's homestead exemption is $50,000, the judgment lien is impairing your exemption - without the lien, you would have $50,000 in exempt equity. Section 522(f) allows you to avoid that judgment lien to protect your exemption.

Liens by Bankruptcy Chapter

Chapter 7

Chapter 7 offers the fewest tools for dealing with liens. The Supreme Court's decision in Dewsnup v. Timm (1992) held that Section 506(d) cannot be used to strip liens in Chapter 7, which means lien stripping is unavailable. However, Chapter 7 debtors can still avoid judicial liens under Section 522(f), redeem personal property under Section 722, and benefit from the trustee's avoidance powers.

In practice, most Chapter 7 debtors deal with liens by either reaffirming the debt (agreeing to remain personally liable in exchange for keeping the property), surrendering the property, or simply continuing to make payments after discharge without reaffirming (sometimes called "ride-through" where permitted by local practice).

Chapter 13

Chapter 13 provides the most powerful lien modification tools available to individual debtors. Lien stripping allows removal of wholly unsecured junior mortgages. Cramdown under Section 506(a) reduces secured claims on personal property (most commonly cars) to current market value. And the Chapter 13 plan itself can restructure payment terms on most secured debts.

The combination of these tools is one of the primary reasons debtors choose Chapter 13 over Chapter 7, even though Chapter 13 requires a 3-to-5-year repayment plan. A debtor with an underwater second mortgage and an upside-down car loan can potentially save tens of thousands of dollars through lien stripping and cramdown. Learn more about eligibility requirements at 1328f.com.

Chapter 11

Chapter 11 provides even broader lien modification authority than Chapter 13, including the ability to cram down secured claims on the debtor's principal residence (the anti-modification clause of Section 1322(b)(2) does not apply in Chapter 11). However, Chapter 11 is more expensive, more complex, and is primarily used by businesses and high-income individuals who exceed the Chapter 13 debt limits.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do liens survive bankruptcy discharge?

Yes. A bankruptcy discharge eliminates personal liability (in personam) but does not automatically remove liens (in rem rights). A creditor with a valid lien can still enforce it against the property even after discharge, unless the lien is stripped, crammed down, or avoided through specific bankruptcy procedures.

What is lien stripping in Chapter 13?

Lien stripping allows a Chapter 13 debtor to remove a junior mortgage (such as a second mortgage or HELOC) when the home's value is less than the balance owed on the senior mortgage. The junior lien is reclassified as unsecured debt and may be discharged at the end of the plan. This is only available in Chapter 13, not Chapter 7.

What is a cramdown in bankruptcy?

A cramdown under 11 U.S.C. Section 506(a) allows the court to bifurcate a secured claim into a secured portion (equal to the property's current value) and an unsecured portion (the difference). The debtor pays only the secured portion at the contract rate or a court-approved rate. This is most commonly used for car loans in Chapter 13.

What is lien avoidance under Section 522(f)?

Section 522(f) allows a debtor to avoid (remove) a judicial lien that impairs an exemption the debtor would otherwise be entitled to claim. This applies to judgment liens on a home or other exempt property. The debtor must file a motion with the bankruptcy court and demonstrate that the lien impairs their exemption using a specific statutory formula.

Can I remove a car lien in bankruptcy?

You cannot remove a car lien entirely, but you may be able to reduce it through cramdown under Section 506(a). If your car is worth less than you owe, the court can reduce the secured claim to the car's current value. However, the 910-day rule (the hanging paragraph of Section 1325(a)) prevents cramdown on vehicles purchased within 910 days before filing.
