Judgment Lien Avoidance -- Section 522(f)

Section 522(f) allows you to remove judicial liens that impair your exemptions. One of the most powerful tools in bankruptcy.

How Section 522(f) Works

Section 522(f)(1)(A) allows a debtor to avoid a judicial lien on property to the extent that the lien impairs an exemption the debtor would otherwise be entitled to claim.

11 U.S.C. Section 522(f)(1): The debtor may avoid the fixing of a lien on an interest of the debtor in property to the extent that such lien impairs an exemption to which the debtor would have been entitled.

Key limitation: Section 522(f) only applies to judicial liens (from lawsuits) and certain nonpossessory, nonpurchase money security interests in specified property. It does not apply to mortgages, car loans, or tax liens.

The Impairment Formula

A lien impairs an exemption if the sum of the following exceeds the property's value:

  1. The judicial lien being challenged
  2. All other liens on the property
  3. The exemption the debtor could claim if there were no liens

Formula: If (judicial lien + all other liens + exemption) > property value, then the judicial lien impairs the exemption to the extent of the excess.

Example:

Home value: $200,000

Mortgage: $160,000

Judgment lien: $25,000

Homestead exemption: $50,000

Total: $160,000 + $25,000 + $50,000 = $235,000

Exceeds value by $35,000. Since the judgment lien is only $25,000, the entire lien can be avoided.

How to File the Motion

  1. Determine the property value -- Get an appraisal or use comparable sales data
  2. Calculate all liens -- Mortgages, other judgment liens, tax liens
  3. Identify your exemption -- The applicable homestead or personal property exemption
  4. Apply the formula -- Demonstrate that the judicial lien impairs the exemption
  5. File a motion in the bankruptcy court with supporting documentation
  6. Serve the judgment creditor -- The creditor has the right to respond and request a hearing

After the Lien Is Avoided

Once the court enters an order avoiding the lien, you should:

  • Record the avoidance order in the county recorder's office where the judgment was recorded
  • Verify title records show the lien is removed
  • Keep copies of the avoidance order permanently

The effect is permanent. Once avoided, the lien does not come back -- even if the underlying debt is not discharged. The lien is removed from the property regardless of the debt's dischargeability.
