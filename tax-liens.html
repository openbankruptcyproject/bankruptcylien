Tax Liens in Bankruptcy

Tax liens are among the most persistent liens in bankruptcy. Personal liability can be eliminated, but the lien stays on your property.

Tax Liens vs. Tax Debts

This distinction is critical for understanding how bankruptcy affects taxes:

ConceptWhat It IsEffect of Discharge
Tax debt (in personam)Your personal obligation to payMay be eliminated if taxes meet discharge criteria
Tax lien (in rem)Government's claim on your propertySurvives discharge -- remains on property

Practical impact: Even after discharge eliminates the personal obligation, the IRS or state can still enforce the lien against your property. If you sell the house, the tax lien must be satisfied from the proceeds.

Federal Tax Liens

The IRS files a Notice of Federal Tax Lien (NFTL) when you owe taxes and have not paid after assessment and demand. This lien attaches to all property you own or acquire -- real estate, vehicles, bank accounts, business assets.

In bankruptcy:

  • Priority tax debts (less than 3 years old) must be paid in full through the plan
  • Secured tax claims (backed by a filed lien) are secured to the extent of property value
  • The lien survives discharge even if the underlying tax is dischargeable

Dealing with Tax Liens in Chapter 13

Chapter 13 provides more tools for addressing tax liens than Chapter 7:

  • Pay the secured portion of the tax claim through the plan at present value
  • Treat the excess as unsecured -- If the lien exceeds property value, the excess may be treated as unsecured (and potentially receive less than full payment)
  • Strip the lien upon completion -- If the tax debt is fully dischargeable and the plan pays the secured portion, the lien is extinguished

Chapter 13 advantage: For debtors with tax liens on their property, Chapter 13 provides a structured path to eliminate both the personal liability and the lien over the plan period.

Which Taxes Are Dischargeable?

Income taxes may be dischargeable if they meet all of these criteria:

  1. Three-year rule: The tax return was due more than 3 years before filing
  2. Two-year rule: The tax return was actually filed more than 2 years before filing
  3. 240-day rule: The taxes were assessed more than 240 days before filing
  4. No fraud or willful evasion: The return was not fraudulent, and you did not willfully attempt to evade the tax

Even when the personal liability is dischargeable, the lien survives unless specifically addressed through a Chapter 13 plan.
