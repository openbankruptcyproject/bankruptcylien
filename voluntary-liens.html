Voluntary Liens - Mortgages and Car Loans

Consensual liens that you agreed to cannot be avoided under 522(f). But bankruptcy provides other tools to manage them.

Home›Voluntary Liens in Bankruptcy - Mortgages and Car Loans | bankruptcylien.com›Voluntary Liens - Mortgages and Car Loans

What Are Voluntary Liens?

Voluntary (consensual) liens are security interests you voluntarily granted when you signed a loan agreement. Common examples:

  • Mortgage liens - Securing your home loan
  • Purchase money security interests - Securing a car loan or equipment loan
  • Home equity lines of credit (HELOCs) - Securing a second mortgage
  • Security agreements on personal property - Furniture, electronics, jewelry financed through the store

Because these liens were voluntarily created, Section 522(f) does not apply. You agreed to pledge the property as collateral, and the Bankruptcy Code respects that agreement.

The Anti-Modification Rule

Section 1322(b)(2) - the anti-modification clause - prevents Chapter 13 plans from modifying the rights of holders of claims secured only by the debtor's principal residence. This means:

  • You cannot reduce the interest rate on your first mortgage
  • You cannot extend the mortgage term beyond the original agreement
  • You cannot reduce the principal balance

Exception - lien stripping: If your home is worth less than the first mortgage balance, a junior mortgage (second mortgage, HELOC) is wholly unsecured and can be stripped in Chapter 13. See lien stripping.

Car Loans and the 910-Day Rule

Car loans can be crammed down in Chapter 13 - meaning the secured claim is reduced to the car's current value - but only if the car was purchased more than 910 days (approximately 2.5 years) before filing.

The "910-day rule" (the hanging paragraph of Section 1325(a)) protects recent car lenders from cramdown. If you bought the car within 910 days, you must pay the full contract balance through the plan - not just the car's current value.

Options for Dealing with Voluntary Liens

While you cannot avoid voluntary liens, bankruptcy provides several options:

OptionEffectChapter
ReaffirmationKeep the debt and the propertyChapter 7
SurrenderGive back the property, discharge the debtAll chapters
Ride-throughKeep paying without reaffirming (where available)Chapter 7
CramdownReduce secured claim to property valueChapter 13
Lien strippingRemove wholly unsecured junior mortgageChapter 13
RedemptionPay current value in lump sumChapter 7 (personal property only)
Cure and maintainCure arrears through plan, keep current on paymentsChapter 13
